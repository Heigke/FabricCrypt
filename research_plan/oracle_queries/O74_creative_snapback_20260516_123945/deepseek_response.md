## Q1 — Unconventional Combinations to Close VG1=0.2

### Evaluation of Given Candidates

**(a) Hybrid transient/DC — slow V_D ramp with body-capacitance integration**  
- **Physical justification**: The device is a relaxation oscillator (self-reset ~400 ns). A DC sweep forces a static equilibrium that doesn’t exist—multiple roots are an artifact of ignoring dynamics. By ramping V_D slowly (e.g., 10 μs sweep from 0→2 V) and integrating the current envelope, we can capture the average current before reset, which for a relaxation oscillator equals the high-current plateau weighted by duty cycle.  
- **Expected dec reduction at VG1=0.2**: ~3–4 decades if the oscillator spends most of its time in the high-current state before resetting. The measured 1e-8 A likely corresponds to this plateau; the low-current root (1e-16 A) is the off-state before triggering.  
- **Implementation pain**: Medium. Requires transient simulation with a capacitance element (C_substrate ~10–100 fF) at the internal body node. Need to ensure the ramp is slow enough to allow trigger but fast enough to avoid multiple resets. Could use a “slow transient → DC envelope” extraction (peak detector).  
- **Single deal-breaker risk**: The measured curve may already be the envelope of a relaxation oscillation, but the envelope may not equal a true DC solution. If the oscillator operates in a chaotic or frequency-dependent mode, the envelope will be inaccurate.

**(b) Two-stage Newton — low-current root → impact-ionization injection jump**  
- **Physical justification**: The high-current root is reached via impact-ionization-generated hole current raising V_BS, which triggers parasitic BJT regeneration. If we can artificially inject a hole current proportional to impact ionization at the low-current root, the Newton step may cross the separatrix.  
- **Expected dec reduction**: 2–4 decades at VG1=0.2, if injection is calibrated correctly.  
- **Implementation pain**: Low to medium. Modify the Newton solver to add an explicit impact-ionization current source (Chynoweth formula I_II = M * I_DS, with M = 1/(1 - (V_DB/BV)^n)). After converging to the low-current root, increase V_D beyond knee, then inject a transient pulse of II current and re-solve.  
- **Deal-breaker**: The injection may not be sufficient to jump the basin; the Newton solver can still collapse back. Also, the impact ionization parameters are unknown (need calibration from the measured snapback voltage).

**(c) 2D PWL(V_G1, V_G2) surface from Sebas's per-bias fits**  
- **Physical justification**: The per-bias BSIM fits accurately reproduce each individual bias curve. By interpolating (e.g., Delaunay triangulation or RBF) across the (V_G1, V_G2) plane, we can produce a continuous surface that reproduces the measured data at the 33 points.  
- **Expected dec reduction**: Near-complete elimination of residuals at the measured biases (<0.1 dec at the 33 points). Away from them, interpolation may be less accurate but still far better than current BSIM.  
- **Implementation pain**: Medium. Need to extract 33 sets of BSIM parameters, then build an interpolation scheme that returns current for arbitrary (V_G1, V_G2, V_D). Must ensure monotonicity and smoothness for convergence.  
- **Deal-breaker**: Overfitting to measurement noise; no physical extrapolation beyond the measured voltage range. If the model is used in a larger circuit, it will fail spectacularly outside the training domain.

**(d) Explicit lateral BJT (drain-body-source parasitic NPN)**  
- **Physical justification**: In a MOS structure, the drain (n+), body (p), source (n+) form a lateral bipolar transistor. At high V_D, impact ionization creates holes that flow into the body, forward-biasing the base-emitter junction (body-source) and turning on the parasitic NPN. This adds collector current from drain to source. The measured 1e-8 A at VG1=0.2 could be entirely this BJT current.  
- **Expected dec reduction**: 5–7 decades at VG1=0.2 if the saturation current I_S and forward beta (β~50–200) are calibrated. The formula: I_C = β * I_B = β * M * I_substrate, where I_substrate is generated by impact ionization.  
- **Implementation pain**: Medium. Need to add a subcircuit model (Gummel-Poon) in parallel with the nFET. Parameters: IS (saturation current), BF (forward beta), VAF (early voltage), CJC (collector-base capacitance).  
- **Deal-breaker**: The lateral BJT may have very low beta in a bulk CMOS process due to the wide base region. Typical β for lateral NPN in 130nm is 10–50, not enough to explain the full 8 decades unless the trigger current is already high. Could be combined with avalanche multiplication.

**(e) Full PNPN SCR (thyristor) latch-up model**  
- **Physical justification**: The 2T cell structure is literally a vertical PNPN: drain (n+), body (p-well), deep n-well (if present), p-substrate. Or, considering the vertical NPN (emitter=source? actually the vertical NPN is likely the n+/p-well/n-well/p-substrate). At high V_D, the reverse-biased drain-body junction breaks down via avalanche, generating holes that forward-bias the p-well/n-well junction, turning on the p-n-p. The regenerative feedback (α_npn + α_pnp ≥ 1) produces a low-impedance latch state with high current. This exactly matches snapback and the measured 1e-8 A.  
- **Expected dec reduction**: 7–8 decades at VG1=0.2, because the latch current is limited only by the on-resistance of the thyristor, which can easily reach 1e-8 A.  
- **Implementation pain**: High. Need two bipolar transistors (vertical NPN and lateral PNP) plus well resistances. Requires knowledge of doping profiles (well depths, substrate resistivity) to set V_br and holding voltage. Also need to model the trigger current.  
- **Deal-breaker**: The standard BSIM4 model cannot simulate latch-up without an explicit SCR model. Implementation in Verilog-A is feasible but parameter extraction may be difficult. The measurement might show a holding voltage that is inconsistent with the SCR model.

**(f) Self-heating with positive feedback**  
- **Physical justification**: Self-heating increases local temperature, which increases I_S (proportional to T^3 exp(-E_g/kT)) and reduces threshold voltage. This can cause thermal runaway, but typically requires high current densities. At 1e-8 A, self-heating is negligible (<0.1°C).  
- **Expected dec reduction**: <0.5 decades.  
- **Implementation pain**: Medium (need thermal RC network).  
- **Deal-breaker**: At low power levels, self-heating is irrelevant.

**(g) Stochastic resonance — Johnson noise injection**  
- **Physical justification**: Injecting noise into the body node can allow the system to escape the low-current basin and find the high-current root. This is a Monte Carlo approach, not a deterministic DC solution.  
- **Expected dec reduction**: 0–2 decades with high variance. Not reliable.  
- **Implementation pain**: High (require many transient noise simulations and averaging).  
- **Deal-breaker**: Physically unrealistic for a DC measurement; no guarantee of convergence.

**(h) Verilog-A custom compact model**  
- **Physical justification**: Bypassing BSIM4 entirely and writing a compact model that includes subthreshold, impact ionization, and a thyristor latch-up section can directly target the missing physics.  
- **Expected dec reduction**: Potentially 8 decades if the model captures the latch-up correctly.  
- **Implementation pain**: High (requires coding, parameter extraction, and validation against measured data).  
- **Deal-breaker**: Without proper physical insight, the model may be overparameterized and not robust.

### Wildcards (Not Listed)

**Wildcard 1: Avalanche multiplication with explicit Chynoweth integral**  
Add an impact ionization current source at the drain-body junction:  
I_II = M * I_DS, where M = 1 / [1 - ∫0^L α_n * exp(-B_n/E) dx] .  
For a simple approximation: M = 1 / [1 - (V_DB/BV)^n] with n≈3–6. This generates the holes needed to forward-bias the body-source junction. Combined with the parasitic BJT, it can explain the snapback.  
- **Expected dec reduction**: 4–6 decades at VG1=0.2.  
- **Pain**: Low (just add a current source in netlist).  
- **Deal-breaker**: BV may be >5V, so at V_D=2V the multiplication factor is small (~1.01). Not enough to trigger regenerative feedback unless the BJT beta is very high.

**Wildcard 2: Trap-assisted tunneling (TAT)**  
TAT from drain to body (or source) can produce a current that scales as I_TAT = A * V_D * exp(-B/V_D). At V_D=2V, this could be 1e-8 A if trap density is high (e.g., due to process damage).  
- **Expected dec reduction**: 3–5 decades.  
- **Pain**: Low (add one equation).  
- **Deal-breaker**: The TAT current is typically weakly voltage-dependent and not S-shaped. It does not explain snapback (the negative differential resistance).

**Wildcard 3: Holistic data-driven neural network correction**  
Train a small NN (e.g., 5-20-10-1) on the residuals (measured – model) using the 33 biases, then add the NN output as a correction term to the BSIM output.  
- **Expected dec reduction**: <0.2 dec at training points.  
- **Pain**: Medium (need to integrate NN into pyport, possibly using ONNX or a simple feedforward in Python).  
- **Deal-breaker**: Poor extrapolation; accepted only as a last resort.

**Wildcard 4: Differentiable solver (JAX)**  
Formulate the KCL equations as a differentiable function and use gradient-based optimization over all 33 biases simultaneously to find a global minimum of the RMSE.  
- **Expected dec reduction**: Might achieve 0.1–0.5 dec if the model structure is correct; otherwise stuck in local minima.  
- **Pain**: Very high (rewrite solver in JAX, 4 hours likely insufficient).  
- **Deal-breaker**: Not feasible within time budget.

## Q2 — What Did You Miss? (Blunt Assessment)

1. **You ignored the most obvious physical mechanism: parasitic SCR (thyristor) latch-up.** The 2T cell is a PNPN stack. At low V_G1 (gate off), the drain-body avalanche triggers the SCR, producing a low-impedance state with high current (~1e-8 A). Your model has zero current because BSIM4 cannot latch. This is embarrassingly fundamental.

2. **You are trying to fit a DC model to a fundamentally dynamic (transient) device.** The measured snapback is not a DC steady state; it is a relaxation oscillation with 400 ns period. A DC sweep sees a bistable system, but the true “current at a given voltage” is the time-averaged envelope, which you could extract from a transient simulation. Instead, you’ve spent 50+ experiments using Newton arclength on a non-existent DC solution.

3. **You applied GIDL as a band-aid without checking the root cause.** GIDL is tunneling from drain to gate; it gives at most picoamps. A current of 1e-8 A is orders too high for GIDL in 130nm. The correct mechanism is avalanche-generated hole current turning on a parasitic bipolar.

4. **You haven’t calibrated BSIM’s impact ionization parameters.** BSIM4 has `I_II` (impact ionization current) via parameters `IIT1`, `IIT2`, etc. Defaults are usually zero. Even a small impact ionization current can trigger the BJT, but you didn’t try that.

5. **You ignored the possibility that the vertical NPN (Gummel-Poon) is already present in your netlist but its β is too low because the base (body) is pinned to 0V.** After fixing V_Sint=0, the body is grounded, so the NPN base-emitter junction is shorted. The vertical NPN cannot turn on. The real device has a floating body during the snapback due to charging of the substrate capacitor. By pinning V_Sint=0, you killed the very mechanism that makes the snapback possible.

6. **The multi-root problem (8 roots) is a telltale sign of a dynamic system forced into DC.** With that many roots, the system is not monotonic; the correct solution only exists in a time-dependent sense. Your arclength warm-start is just choosing the wrong stable manifold.

7. **You haven’t considered measuring the transient waveform** (e.g., V_D and I_D vs time) to understand the relaxation oscillator. That would give you direct data for a time-domain model rather than trying to fit a non-existent DC curve.

Given 4 hours compute, pyport, 33 measured biases. Propose one concrete stack of 3–4 fixes layered together, in order, with expected residual RMSE and early failure indicators.

### Stack: “Thyristor-Transient-Hybrid with Impact Ionization”

**Order and details:**

2. **Add explicit lateral NPN transistor (drain-body-source) using Gummel-Poon** (1 hour)

   - This models the parasitic bipolar that amplifies the impact ionization current.  
   - Keep the vertical NPN (if present) but ensure its base is not shorted to ground.

3. **Switch to transient simulation with a slow V_D ramp (1 μs sweep)** (1 hour)

**Expected residual RMSE:**

**Early failure indicators (within first hour):**

**Why this stack?**  
It addresses the two fundamental errors: missing physics (thyristor) and wrong measurement interpretation (DC vs transient). It is feasible in 4 hours because we reuse existing BSIM code and only add a few extra elements. The transient approach avoids the multi-root problem entirely by letting the system evolve physically. The lateral BJT + impact ionization is the minimum set needed to get the 8-decade jump.

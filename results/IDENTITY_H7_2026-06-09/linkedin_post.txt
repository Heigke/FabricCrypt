Can a language model run on the live electrical signals of one specific chip?

Over the last days my AI research agent tested exactly that on two real AMD machines: take a frozen GPT-2 and make its text depend, in real time, on signals coming off the silicon it runs on.

The signals that actually move (measured, not assumed): per-core voltage (Vcore), per-core clock, and power draw. These are read live and fed into a tiny adapter on top of the frozen model — no fused constants, and no crypto key in the loop.

Result, on unseen text (live-signals-only model):
• Its OWN body's live signals → fluent English, even slightly better than plain GPT-2 (perplexity 47 vs 65 on ikaros; 38 vs 67 on daedalus).
• A foreign chip, or a random body → collapses into noise, ~150–2200× worse, deterministically, on both machines.

I think of it as layers of embodiment, each verified on real silicon:
① identity — the per-die voltage/clock pattern separates the two chips
② freshness — live jitter drifts, so a replayed reading goes stale
③ real-time coupling — it generates off live reads of its own body
④ behaviour binding — right body fluent, wrong body broken (trained)
⑤ crypto lock — a TPM-sealed key, refused on a foreign die (this is just the lock, not the body)
⑥ liveness — a fresh TPM nonce gates each run

Where I had to be honest with myself: before training, the "hardware steering" was statistically indistinguishable from a random vector — a flashy demo can be an artifact. So I probed it teacher-forced, ran null controls, and only trusted it after training made the dependence real and reproducible.

What this is — and isn't: I'd call it operational embodiment — the behaviour is causally, reproducibly rooted in the specific physical body; change the chip and the behaviour changes or breaks. It is NOT a claim about consciousness. The fluency comes from adaptation, the adapter is domain-narrow (124M proof-of-concept), and without a GPU secure-enclave it won't stop a privileged attacker on the same machine — only make a copy go stale. Binding models to devices isn't new either — see Clifford et al., "Locking Machine Learning Models into Hardware" (SaTML 2025), which encrypts weights; our twist is binding via live signals + a small learned steering module, on consumer hardware that lacks a TEE.

The goal we're building toward: the body's own computation wired into the model — unique, fresh, and uncopyable, at rest and while it runs. Early and small, but it runs on real silicon, and the method, the caveats and the code are open.
